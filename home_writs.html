Writs

A writ is an order or formal document issued by a court or other legally recognized body, instructing an individual to take some action or not perform certain actions. The writ has been a necessary element in jurisdictions based upon common law, especially for the administration of justice, the enforcement of decisions provided by courts of law, or for special legal services. Writs can be broadly categorized into different types, each serving a different purpose.

The Writ of Habeas Corpus: is a legal tool used to protect people's freedom from being held without good reason. It is one of the most important protections of personal freedom in legal systems based on common law.

The writ primarily serves to prevent detention that is either illegal or unjust. It ensures that no person is deprived of his or her liberty without the process of law and also it acts as a check on the powers of police and government officers.

The court issues the writ to the person or authority holding the convict . The convict must be brought to the court by the custodian and justify the detention. The court examines whether the detention was lawful.

Suspension During Emergencies: The right to habeas corpus can be suspended during emergencies or martial law.

National Security Exceptions: Restrictions may apply in cases involving national security or foreign detainees.

Post-Conviction Limitations: Limited scope for filing after a final criminal conviction
